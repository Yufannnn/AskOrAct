\section*{What Has Been Done}

All milestones through March 13 are complete.
We implement a cooperative $9{\times}9$ two-room gridworld in which a principal holds a hidden goal and issues an ambiguous instruction mapping to $K \in \{1,2,3,4\}$ candidate goals.
The assistant maintains a Bayesian posterior over goals, initialised from the instruction and updated by observed principal actions via a Boltzmann likelihood~\cite{baker2009,jara2016}, and at each step compares the expected team cost of acting now versus asking the best available clarifying question~\cite{hadfield2016cirl}:
\[
  \text{CostAsk}(q) = 1 + c_q + \mathbb{E}_{a|q}[V(s,b_{q,a})], \;\;
  \text{CostAct} = 1 + V(s,b,g^*).
\]
Seven policies were implemented: \textit{AskOrAct} (VoI), \textit{NeverAsk}, \textit{AlwaysAsk}, \textit{InfoGainAsk}, \textit{EasyInfoGainAsk}, \textit{RandomAsk}, and \textit{POMCPPlanner}.
A full sweep over $\varepsilon \in \{0,0.05,0.1\}$, $\beta \in \{1,2,4\}$, 5 replicate seeds $\times$ 20 episodes (100 per cell, 19{,}800 total) was run with 95\% bootstrap CIs.
Ablations (question cost, wrong-pick failure mode, deadline margin), robustness sweeps (answer noise, principal $\beta$-mismatch), held-out template generalization, and scale-$K$ stress tests ($K$ up to 6) were completed.

\textbf{Bug discovered and fixed.}
\textit{AskOrAct} produced results identical to \textit{NeverAsk} at $K{=}2$ (zero questions asked).
The cause: \texttt{ENTROPY\_GATE = 0.8}, but the uniform prior over $K{=}2$ goals has entropy $\ln 2 \approx 0.693 < 0.8$, so the gate fired at every step.
Fix: \texttt{ENTROPY\_GATE} reduced from 0.8 to 0.3.
Post-fix: \textit{AskOrAct} 97\% success vs.\ \textit{NeverAsk} 85\% at $K{=}2$ ($\varepsilon{=}0$, $\beta{=}1$).

\section*{Preliminary Findings}

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{main_dashboard.png}
  \caption{Main evaluation dashboard (100 ep/cell, error bars = 95\% CI).}
  \label{fig:main}
\end{figure}

Figure~\ref{fig:main} and Tables~\ref{tab:k3}--\ref{tab:k2} summarise key results.
At $K{=}1$ all policies are equivalent (sanity check).
At $K \geq 3$, \textit{AskOrAct} outperforms \textit{NeverAsk} with statistical significance:
$+7.9$\,pp at $K{=}3$ (95\% CI $[5.0, 10.8]$) and $+8.4$\,pp at $K{=}4$ (95\% CI $[5.1, 12.1]$), while asking only 0.6--0.7 questions per episode.
\textit{AlwaysAsk} asks 3--4$\times$ more but achieves similar or lower success and much higher regret ($+2.1$ at $K{=}4$).
Robustness sweeps show the advantage holds under answer noise (K=4: $+4$--$+9$\,pp across noise 0--0.2) and principal model mismatch ($+13.5$\,pp when $\beta_{\text{true}}{=}1$ vs.\ $\hat\beta{=}2$).
The ablation confirms that a non-zero question cost ($c_q{=}0.5$) is essential: setting $c_q{=}0$ causes over-asking, reducing success from 73.8\% to 69.2\%.

\section*{Key Plot/Table for Final Report}

\textbf{Figure~\ref{fig:main} (main dashboard)} will appear in the final report.
The horizontal axis is ambiguity $K \in \{1,2,3,4\}$; the three vertical panels show (top) success rate, (middle) regret, and (bottom) questions asked per episode, one bar group per policy with 95\% CI error bars.
Readers learn: (a) at $K{=}1$ all policies tie---confirming the implementation is correct; (b) the success gap between \textit{AskOrAct} and \textit{NeverAsk} widens monotonically with $K$; (c) \textit{AlwaysAsk} asks many more questions without a commensurate success gain, visible from the contrast between panels (top) and (bottom).
This figure is the central evidence for the claim that selective asking is superior to both extremes.

\textbf{Aggregate delta table} (to appear in final report): rows are $K \in \{2,3,4\}$, columns are $\Delta$Success (\textit{AskOrAct}$-$\textit{NeverAsk}) with 95\% CI, $\Delta$Regret (\textit{AskOrAct}$-$\textit{AlwaysAsk}), and questions/ep.
Readers see at a glance whether improvements are statistically significant and at what cost in questions.
