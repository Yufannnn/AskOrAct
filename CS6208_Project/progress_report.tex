\section*{System Overview}

We implement a cooperative gridworld where a principal holds a hidden goal (which object to fetch) and issues a short, potentially ambiguous instruction.
The assistant observes the state, the instruction, and the principal's movement actions, and at each step must choose between a physical task action or a clarifying question.

Goal inference follows Bayesian inverse planning \cite{baker2009}: we maintain a posterior $b(g)$ over candidate goals, initialized from the instruction template and updated via a Boltzmann action likelihood $P(a \mid s, g) \propto \exp(-\beta \cdot d(s', g))$ with uniform noise rate $\varepsilon$ \cite{jara2016}.
The ask-or-act policy selects the option with lower expected team cost:
\[
  \text{cost}_{\text{ask}}(q) = 1 + c_q + \mathbb{E}_{a \mid q}[V(s, b_{q,a})], \quad
  \text{cost}_{\text{act}} = 1 + V(s, b, g^*)
\]
where $c_q$ is the question cost, $b_{q,a}$ is the posterior after answer $a$, and $V$ approximates remaining distance to the MAP goal.
This is a value-of-information (VoI) framing, connecting to cooperative inverse reinforcement learning \cite{hadfield2016cirl} and cooperative language-guided inverse planning \cite{tan2024clips}.
Language is a controlled discrete channel: templated instructions map deterministically to candidate goal sets; questions and answers are drawn from a fixed vocabulary.

\section*{What Was Implemented}

\begin{itemize}\setlength{\itemsep}{1pt}
  \item 9$\times$9 two-room gridworld with 6 objects and BFS pathfinding.
  \item Four instruction templates producing $K \in \{1, 2, 3, 4\}$ candidate goals.
  \item Boltzmann principal with parameters $\beta$ (rationality) and $\varepsilon$ (noise).
  \item Bayesian posterior: initialized from instruction, updated from principal actions.
  \item Question bank: three question types (color, room, object type) with per-type answer noise.
  \item Seven assistant policies: \textit{AskOrAct} (VoI), \textit{NeverAsk}, \textit{AlwaysAsk}, \textit{InfoGainAsk}, \textit{EasyInfoGainAsk}, \textit{RandomAsk}, \textit{POMCPPlanner}.
  \item Full evaluation sweep: $K \in \{1,2,3,4\}$, $\varepsilon \in \{0, 0.05, 0.1\}$, $\beta \in \{1, 2, 4\}$, 5 replicate seeds $\times$ 20 episodes per condition (100 episodes per cell), 95\% bootstrap CIs.
  \item Ablation suite: question cost weighting (0 vs.\ 0.5), wrong-pick failure mode, episode deadline margin (3/5/7 steps).
  \item Robustness sweeps: answer noise variation, principal $\beta$-mismatch.
  \item Held-out instruction template generalization and scale-$K$ stress test ($K$ up to 6).
\end{itemize}

\section*{Bug Discovered and Fixed}

During analysis we found that \textit{AskOrAct} produced results identical to \textit{NeverAsk} at $K{=}2$, with zero questions asked across all episodes.
The cause was \texttt{ENTROPY\_GATE = 0.8} in \texttt{src/config.py}.
The policy skips the ask branch when $H(b) \leq \texttt{ENTROPY\_GATE}$, but the uniform prior over $K{=}2$ goals has entropy $\ln 2 \approx 0.693 < 0.8$, so the gate fires at every step and asking is never considered.

\textbf{Fix:} \texttt{ENTROPY\_GATE} reduced from \textbf{0.8} to \textbf{0.3}.
This value lies below $\ln 2$ (allowing K=2 to enter the ask branch) and above the near-certain threshold ($\approx 0.05$--$0.10$), letting the expected-cost comparison determine whether to ask.
Three additional minor fixes were applied: a guard against \texttt{nan} probabilities when all goal positions are unreachable in \texttt{principal\_action\_probs}; \texttt{float("nan")} sentinels in the degenerate-episode return path of the evaluation loop; and a warning print when a policy requests an unregistered question ID.

\section*{Robustness and Generalization}

\textbf{Answer noise.}
Sweeping per-question answer noise from 0 to 0.2 shows that \textit{AskOrAct}'s advantage over \textit{NeverAsk} is retained in most conditions.
At $K{=}4$, the success gap holds across all noise levels (+4--9\,pp).
At $K{=}3$ the advantage is smaller and reverses at noise$\,{=}\,0.1$ (\textit{AskOrAct} 92.0\% vs.\ \textit{NeverAsk} 94.0\%), where noisy answers can mislead the posterior; the gap re-opens at noise$\,{=}\,0.2$ (95.0\% vs.\ 94.0\%).

\textbf{Principal model mismatch.}
The assistant's model is fixed at $\hat{\beta}{=}2$ while the principal's true $\beta$ is varied over $\{1, 2, 4\}$.
The hardest condition is true $\beta{=}1$ (principal acts near-randomly; parameter distance $|\beta_{\text{true}}-\hat{\beta}|{=}1$), where \textit{AskOrAct} retains a +13.5\,pp advantage over \textit{NeverAsk} (83.5\% vs.\ 70.0\%); at matched $\beta{=}2$ the gap is +4\,pp (92.5\% vs.\ 88.5\%).
The largest parameter mismatch ($\beta_{\text{true}}{=}4$, distance${=}2$) is less disruptive because a highly rational principal is easier to model.

\textbf{Held-out instruction templates.}
Testing on instruction templates not seen during policy design shows performance degrades mildly but maintains the policy ordering, confirming that results are not template-specific.

\textbf{Scale-$K$.}
Extending to $K \in \{5, 6\}$ shows the advantage of asking grows: at $K{=}6$, \textit{AskOrAct} retains meaningful success rates while \textit{NeverAsk} falls substantially, consistent with VoI being most valuable under high ambiguity.
